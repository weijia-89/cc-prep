What's missing on purpose

  • Nothing about learning styles. The meshing hypothesis (Pashler and colleagues 2008) didn't survive replication, and pairing a diagram with text helps everyone whether they self-identify as a "visual learner" or not.
  • No re-reading prompts. A re-read feels like studying because the material gets fluent on the page, but Koriat and Bjork showed that fluency on the page isn't the same as recall under pressure. When you want to review, the flashcards are the review.
  • No 10,000-hour gestures. Macnamara and colleagues 2014 found deliberate practice accounted for somewhere around 12% of variance in education contexts, which is real but a long way from the pop-science framing. Thirty focused minutes with feedback beats two hours scrolling.
  • No advice about studying in conditions that match the test center. The context-dependent learning effect exists (Smith and Vela 2001 put d at roughly 0.28) and isn't worth chasing.

A few rules that earn back time

  • Don't load new material in the last two hours before sleep. Slow-wave sleep does most of the work consolidating declarative memory (Diekelmann and Born 2010), and content you saw five minutes before bed mostly hasn't reached the system that consolidates it.
  • Be honest with the confidence ratings on flashcards. The whole rescheduling logic depends on it, and overrating yourself just makes the deck shorter without making you better.
  • By the last week, when you mark something Certain you should be right roughly 95% of the time. If you're averaging 85% confidence and 80% accuracy, the fix isn't "study more material," it's working out which items you actually know versus which ones just look familiar.
  • When the question stem uses BEST, MOST, FIRST, or NEXT, two of the four options will look fine. Re-read the qualifier. The intended answer almost always lines up with a published framework, the IR lifecycle or the CIA triad or defense in depth, rather than a tool name.
  • NOT and EXCEPT items reverse the logic. Restate the stem as "which one is false" before you look at the choices, because the most familiar-sounding answer will otherwise pull you straight to the wrong selection.

1.1 The in practice

On the exam: Stems describe an incident; you pick which leg of the CIA triad broke — often two at once.

Core idea

Confidentiality = who can read it. Integrity = whether it changed. Availability = whether you can use it when needed.

Analogy

A hospital chart: locked chart room (C), tamper-evident seals on pages (I), the ward stays open during visiting hours (A).

Mnemonic · story/link [verified: Dunlosky — pair with practice items]

“Read the letter, check it wasn’t altered, make sure the mailbox opens.” Letter = confidentiality (only the right reader). Altered = integrity. Mailbox opens on schedule = availability. On the exam, run the story backward: describe the attack, name the broken leg.

Most exam items on the triad don't ask you to recite , integrity, and . They give you a scenario, usually some attack happening to a real-sounding system, and they want you to say which of those three properties got broken. Plenty of real-world incidents break two at once, and the exam will sometimes test that as well, which is part of why scenario-mapping practice matters more than memorizing the definitions.

ExampleIf you were running a reporting pipeline where you validate against source-of-truth exports you are allowed to see — not raw prod tables, you'd see all three properties show up in concrete decisions. Keeping subscriber segments out of screenshots and tickets you are not cleared to file is the story, and you'd handle it with encrypted volumes and an auth layer on the endpoint. Worrying that a dashboard tile shows the wrong date range after a deploy is the story, since a tampered-with embedding is, structurally, just data tampering with extra steps. And the reason a nightly export job stalls turns into a blank chart for marketers for whoever's calling the service is , which is why people put hot-standby into the design at all.
Where the analogy breaks

The triad is useful as a lens, but treating it as the checklist will cost you on the exam. Privacy, authenticity, and non-repudiation are their own concepts and they show up alongside CIA, not inside it. If a question asks about a digital signature, the right answer probably isn't going to be one of the three letters.

1.2 IAAA

In context

Builds on 1.1 CIA: once you know which property broke, ask whether IAAA failed before or after the attack.

On the exam: MFA traps: two passwords are still one factor. Non-repudiation questions want signatures, not shared secrets.

Core idea

Identify (claim) → authenticate (prove) → authorize (allowed actions) → accountability (prove who did it).

Analogy

Concert venue: show ID at the door (identify), wristband scan (authenticate), VIP vs GA section (authorize), ticket tied to your name in the log (accountability).

Identification
claiming an identity. A username, email, badge ID. It's a claim, not yet proof.
Authentication
proving the claim. Three factor categories: knowledge (password), possession (token, phone), inherence (biometric). means at least two different categories. Two passwords are not .
Authorization

Accountability
tying actions back to a user. It needs non-repudiable logs and unique IDs. No shared accounts.
ExampleWalking through IAAA on a shared campaign doc: your Google account name is identification (a claim). Password plus phone prompt is authentication — two factor categories if the second factor is not another password. Google sets viewer vs commenter vs editor — authorization. Workspace audit log and version history are accountability; shared “team@” logins break the chain the same way shared root would on a server.

Digital signatures get you there because the private key only one party holds; tamper-evident logs back that up. crypto can't, because either side could have generated the same , so neither one can prove the other did it.

1.3 management and the formulas worth memorizing

On the exam: Quantitative items use SLE and ALE; qualitative items ask mitigate vs transfer vs accept vs avoid.

Core idea

Risk is threat × vulnerability × impact in stories, but the math you compute is SLE = AV × EF and ALE = SLE × ARO.

Analogy

Home insurance:

Mnemonic · acronym + story — risk treatments

AMTA: Avoid (cancel the risky launch), Mitigate (add controls — peer QA gates on a GTM), Transfer (insurance / vendor owns the stack), Accept (residual within appetite). Exam trap: “buy insurance” = transfer, not mitigate.

you don't eliminate fire (threat), you reduce damage (mitigate), buy a policy (transfer), or accept that the shed isn't worth sprinklers (accept).

The textbook expression of risk is something like times vulnerability times impact, but you should treat that as conceptual rather than something you'd actually plug numbers into. The workflow is what matters: identify what could go wrong, decide how big a deal it would be, pick how you're going to handle it. The formulas the exam does want you to compute are SLE and ALE, both of which are downstream of "what is this asset worth and how often does the bad thing happen."

Quantitative formulas (memorize): SLE = AV × EF ; single loss expectancy = asset value × exposure factor ALE = SLE × ARO ; annualized loss expectancy = SLE × annualized rate of occurrence ROI = (ALE_before - ALE_after) - control_cost

1.4 The two ways the exam classifies controls

In context

Builds on 1.3: every control you pick has both a category (what it is) and a function (what it does).

On the exam: One control, two axes: category (admin / technical / physical) and function (prevent / detect / correct…). CCTV is the classic multi-function trap.

Core idea

Category asks what kind of thing it is; function asks when it acts relative to the incident.

Analogy

Home security: deadbolt (prevent), doorbell cam recording (detect), calling a locksmith after a break-in (corrective). Same house, different jobs.

Controls get classified two ways at once. A fence is a physical control by category and a deterrent plus a preventive control by function. The exam will often ask both questions in the same item.

The category axis (administrative, technical, physical) is asking what kind of thing the control is. A locked door is physical regardless of what it's protecting. A rule is technical regardless of what it's blocking. A "no shared accounts" is administrative regardless of who enforces it. Picture a single security review of an office: walking the hallway, you see physical controls (locked doors, badge readers, the receptionist watching the lobby). Sitting at a workstation, you see technical controls (the , the antivirus, the disk encryption). Reading the wiki, you see administrative controls (the AUP everyone signed, the plan, the training schedule). Same building, three lenses.

The function axis is asking what the control does in time, relative to a security event happening. A preventive control stops the event before it occurs — a blocking a port, a lock on a door, on a login. A detective control notices that something happened — an alert, a log entry, a CCTV recording someone's already on the floor. A corrective control fixes things after the fact — a patch deployed after a was exploited, a restore from backup after a ransomware . A deterrent doesn't physically stop anyone, but it changes the attacker's calculus before they try (the visible camera, the warning sign, the security guard standing at the entrance). A recovery control gets operations back to normal (the DR site coming online, the failover firing). A compensating control is the alternative you put in place when the primary control isn't feasible (a manual review process when you can't deploy automated DLP yet). And a directive control tells people what to do (signage, the AUP itself, the document).

The exam pattern that catches people: a single control usually fills more than one function. Watch CCTV across an event. While it's just sitting there visible, it's deterrent (the would-be attacker sees it and goes elsewhere). While someone is actively monitoring it, it's detective (the SOC notices a person in a hallway after hours). After the incident is over and law enforcement subpoenas the recording, it became corrective in service of the investigation, and the recording itself is evidence. Same camera, three different functions depending on the moment. So when a question asks "CCTV is what kind of control," the right answer often depends on the scenario in the stem.

1.5 The ISC2 Code of Ethics, in order

In context

Builds on governance in 1.6 preview: ethics outrank client interest when canons conflict.

On the exam: Canon order is testable: society beats client interest every time.

Core idea

Four canons, numbered. When they clash, the lower number wins.

Analogy

Airline safety rules vs a VIP demanding to skip checks — the public rule wins even if the VIP is paying.

  1. Protect society, the common good, necessary public trust and confidence, and the infrastructure.
  2. Act honorably, honestly, justly, responsibly, and legally.
  3. Provide diligent and competent service to principals.
  4. Advance and protect the profession.

When canons conflict, the lower-numbered one wins. Society outranks honor, which outranks principals, which outranks profession. Your client's interest never overrides society's.

Quick check

Canon 1 in one phrase?

Answer: Protect society, common good, public trust, and infrastructure.

2.1 How BC, DR, and IR fit together

On the exam: Know which program owns ongoing ops vs catastrophic IT loss vs single security events.

Core idea

IR handles one event now; DR restores IT after disaster; BC keeps the business alive through anything.

Analogy

Restaurant: IR is the kitchen fire tonight; DR is rebuilding the kitchen; BC is still serving customers from a food truck.

The three nest. is the smallest scope, dealing with a single event in real time. Disaster recovery is broader and kicks in when something has actually taken IT systems down, whether that's a single nasty incident or a literal disaster. Business continuity is the umbrella over all of it, and it's about keeping the business running regardless of what's caused the disruption, which can include things that have nothing to do with security at all.

Quick check

Smallest scope: BC, DR, or IR?

Answer: IR.

2.2 The BIA

In context

Builds on 2.1 nesting: BIA numbers justify DR/BC spend.

On the exam: BIA drives RTO/RPO — questions ask what document you write first.

Core idea

List critical functions, rank them, put dollars and time on downtime, then set recovery targets.

Analogy

Moving house: decide which rooms you need first (CBF), how long you can camp in the garage (MTD), then buy insurance and boxes.

A Business Impact Analysis is the document everything else hangs off. It works out which business functions are critical, ranks them against each other, and puts a number on what disruption to each one would cost. Once you have that, you can sensibly write the and the DRP, because you actually know what you're protecting and how much it's worth to protect it.

  • Identify critical business functions (CBF)
  • Determine MTD for each CBF (max tolerable downtime)
  • Set and RPO based on MTD and data-loss tolerance
  • Identify dependencies (people, systems, vendors, facilities)
  • Estimate financial and reputational impact over time
Quick check

What does a BIA produce that a DRP needs?

Answer: Prioritized functions, MTD, RTO/RPO, dependencies, impact estimates.

2.5 DR site types, told as the morning your data center caught fire (see diagram)

On the exam: Match site tier to RTO/cost: cold = cheapest, longest RTO; hot = opposite.

Core idea

Hot = live duplicate; warm = hardware ready, data lags; cold = empty floor.

Analogy

4am fire call: hot site means you already moved into the spare house; cold site means you still need to buy furniture.

Picture this scenario, because it makes the categories stop being abstract. It's 4am. Your primary data center is on fire, or flooded, or has lost both power feeds, or has a confirmed outbreak that's locked every server in the rack. The CEO calls you at 4:17am and asks how soon the company can be back online. Your answer depends entirely on which of these site types you decided to pay for last year.

Hot Site

If you have a hot site, your answer is "we're already failing over." A hot site is a fully duplicated production environment, with the same servers, the same storage arrays, and live data replication running constantly. The applications are licensed and warm. Some staff are physically there or on call to flip the switch.

The recovery time is in minutes, sometimes seconds with a properly tested automation. The reason most companies don't have one is the cost: you're paying for two of everything, plus the network bandwidth to keep them in sync, plus a second team to run it. For a financial-trading platform where a minute of downtime is millions of dollars, the math works.

For most organizations, it doesn't.

Warm Site

A warm site is the more common choice. The hardware is racked and powered. The operating systems and base applications are installed.

What's missing is current data: maybe last night's backup is sitting in a restore queue, but the live transactional data from the last few hours isn't there. So when the CEO calls, your answer is "give us four to eight hours to restore the latest backup and verify the application." The cost is meaningfully lower than a hot site because you're not paying for live replication, but you're also not back online while the building is still smoking. Most enterprises run warm sites for their tier-2 systems and hot sites only for the truly critical ones.

Cold Site

A cold site is what it sounds like: an empty facility with power, cooling, and floor space, but no servers and no data. When the CEO calls, your answer is "we're going to need to procure equipment, ship it in, configure it from scratch, and restore data from tape. Two to six weeks." The reason this even exists as an option is that the lease is cheap and a slow recovery is better than no recovery. Small organizations sometimes choose cold sites for compliance reasons, knowing they'd really lose weeks of operations if a disaster hit. The exam likes to ask "which site has the lowest cost and longest " — that's cold.

Mobile Site

A mobile site is a trailer or shipping container outfitted with networking, servers, and HVAC, that gets driven to wherever it's needed. Telcos use them after hurricanes to restore cell coverage. Banks have used them to restore branch operations after building damage. Useful when the disaster is geographic — a tornado wiped out the whole office park — and you need infrastructure on a non-affected piece of land within hours.

Reciprocal / mutual-aid agreement

A reciprocal or mutual-aid agreement is a contract with another organization that says "if your data center goes down, you can use ours, and vice versa." It used to be common between companies in the same industry. The thing the exam wants you to know about reciprocal agreements is that they sound great in theory and tend to fall apart in practice. The partner organization has its own production work running.

Their staff didn't sign up to help you. Capacity is rarely sufficient for two production loads at once. And if a regional disaster hits both of you, the agreement is useless.

Cloud DR Site

A cloud DR site is the modern answer that has displaced most of the others for organizations born after 2010. You keep templates of your infrastructure as code, and your data is replicated to cloud storage. When the disaster hits, you spin up the environment on demand.

You only pay for the running infrastructure when you actually need it, which makes the unit economics enormously better than maintaining a physical warm site. The recovery time depends on how much you've automated: with mature infrastructure-as-code and pre-baked AMIs, it can rival a warm site's hours; without those, it takes longer.

3.4 Authentication factors and what each one really proves

In context

Builds on 3.3: authentication precedes authorization in every story.

On the exam: Factor category beats brand names; smartcard is possession, not inherence.

Core idea

Know / have / are — MFA needs two categories.

Analogy

ATM: PIN (know) + card (have). Fingerprint alone is only one category (are).

Each factor category is answering a different question about the person trying to log in, and each has a way of failing that says something about its limitations.

Knowledge factors answer "do you know a secret?" Passwords, PINs, security questions. They're cheap to deploy and they fail in three predictable ways. People reuse them across sites, so a breach at a small site exposes the password to a bigger one. People can be tricked into revealing them, which is why phishing works. And they can be guessed or brute-forced when the is weak. The reason knowledge alone has fallen out of favor isn't that passwords are bad in principle — it's that humans are bad at managing one secret per service for hundreds of services.

Possession factors answer "do you have a specific physical thing?" A TOTP app on your phone, a YubiKey on your keychain, a smart card in your wallet. The defining property is that the attacker has to physically take it from you, or find a way to make a copy that's harder than just stealing your password. Possession factors have their own failure modes: the YubiKey gets lost (you need a backup), the phone gets stolen (now what), or — the SMS-specific failure — your carrier accepts a "SIM swap" social-engineering call from an attacker, after which your "phone" is the attacker's phone. SIM-swap attacks are why SMS-based is now considered weaker than the alternatives.

Inherence factors answer "are you, biologically, this person?" Fingerprint, face, iris, voice. These feel strong because they're hard to steal in the abstract, and on the exam they're often the right answer when "high security" is in the stem. But they have a category-specific problem: biometrics are not revocable. If a password leaks, you change it. If your fingerprint template leaks, you can't grow a new finger. And every biometric system tunes a tradeoff between FAR (false accept rate, where the wrong person gets in — a security problem) and FRR (false reject rate, where the right person gets locked out — a usability problem). Tighten the threshold to drop FAR and FRR rises; loosen it to help legitimate users and FAR rises. The CER, also called EER, is the threshold where FAR and FRR are equal, and it's the comparison metric across systems: a biometric with a CER of 0.1% is better than one with a CER of 1%.

Location factors ("somewhere you are") and behavior factors ("something you do, like how you type or walk") are usually contextual, not standalone. The bank's app refusing to let you transfer money when you're suddenly logging in from a country you've never been to is a location factor. They're a useful supplement, not a replacement.

, the thing the exam wants you to recognize, requires at least two of these categories, not just two factors. The reason this trips people up is that the question often presents two things that *feel* like different proofs but are both knowledge: a password plus a PIN, or a password plus a security question. Two pieces of knowledge are not , regardless of how clever the second piece is. Password plus fingerprint is . Password plus a code from your authenticator app is . Password plus PIN is just two passwords.

3.6 Physical access, walked from the parking lot inward

In context

Builds on 3.1 defense in depth: physical layers are the outer rings.

On the exam: Physical controls stack from perimeter to interior; mantrap beats a single door.

Core idea

Deter → detect → delay → respond as you move inward.

Analogy

Castle: moat and wall (perimeter), gatehouse (mantrap), guards in the keep (interior).

The cleanest way to remember physical security controls is to walk an attacker from the street to the server, and notice what each layer is doing.

The attacker pulls into the parking lot. The first things they see are deterrents: a chain-link fence with razor wire, "Property of XYZ Corp — patrolled" signs, floodlights pointed at the entrances, visible CCTV domes on the building corners, and a uniformed guard at a desk inside the lobby. None of those things physically *stop* anyone — a determined attacker can climb the fence and ignore the signs — but they raise the perceived cost of the attempt. A casual or opportunistic attacker walks away. That's what a deterrent is for: changing the calculus before the attempt happens.

The attacker is determined and gets to the building. Now they meet preventive controls, the things designed to physically block entry. Bollards stop a vehicle from ramming the lobby. Locks on the doors stop people without keys. The badge reader at the entrance lets in only people whose RFID matches the access-control list. A turnstile gates the flow so two people can't slip through at once on one swipe. And in the most secure version, a mantrap (sometimes called an access-control vestibule) is a small room with two doors, only one of which opens at a time: you scan in, the outer door closes, the system verifies you, and only then does the inner door open. The mantrap exists specifically because the badge reader alone can't tell whether one person walked through or three did. It defeats both tailgating (the follower has no credentials and just walks in behind someone) and piggybacking (the authorized person holds the door — the follower has consent, which is still a breach because the system never verified the follower's identity).

The attacker somehow makes it inside the building. Now detective controls earn their keep. CCTV cameras are recording. Motion sensors fire when someone enters a wing they shouldn't. Door logs show every badge swipe with a timestamp. The attacker may already be in, but the controls now make sure that someone notices and that there's evidence of what happened. Cameras don't physically stop anyone, which is why you don't put them in the preventive bucket. They identify and record.

Floating above all of this is CPTED, Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design. The idea is that the physical layout itself shapes how attackers behave. Sightlines that mean someone walking up to a door is visible from inside the building (natural surveillance). Plantings and signage that make the boundary between public and private space obvious (territorial reinforcement). Pathways that funnel visitors past a reception desk rather than allowing direct access (natural access control). And visible maintenance — clean walkways, working lights, repaired fences — that signals "people are paying attention here," because broken windows attract more breakage. CPTED is the design philosophy that lets the deterrent, preventive, and detective layers actually work; without it, you can stack technology and still create a building that's easy to slip into.

4.2 Ports, told as the story they actually are

In context

Builds on 4.1 layers: ports are how L4 names conversations.

On the exam: Cleartext legacy ports vs TLS ports; SSH on 22 is the odd secure default.

Core idea

Old protocols got low ports; TLS versions got high partners in the same family.

Analogy

Two mail slots: postcard (HTTP 80) vs locked box (HTTPS 443).

Trying to memorize a list of port numbers cold is the worst way to learn ports. The list looks arbitrary, your brain treats it as arbitrary, and you can drill it for hours and still blank on whether IMAP is 143 or 110. The reason ports stick when they stick is that each one has a story attached, and once you know the story the number falls out of it.

Cleartext legacy ports

Start with a small history. The earliest internet protocols were designed in the 1970s and early 1980s, before anyone seriously worried about eavesdropping. So the original mail, file, web, and remote-shell protocols all ran in cleartext, and they got the low, easy-to-remember port numbers.

Port 21 is FTP, 23 is Telnet, 25 is SMTP, 80 is HTTP, 110 is POP3, and 143 is IMAP. Picture each of those as a wide-open postcard: anyone on the path can read whatever you wrote. None of them should be running on the public internet today, and a question that asks "which of these protocols sends credentials in cleartext" almost always has Telnet or unencrypted FTP as the answer for that reason.

TLS on higher ports

When arrived, the encrypted versions were not retrofitted onto the same ports. Each protocol got a *new* port for its variant, usually a much higher number. So HTTPS landed on 443, POP3S on 995, IMAPS on 993, SMTPS on 465, and LDAPS on 636. The pattern that helps here: high port numbers carry encrypted traffic, low port numbers in the same family carry the unencrypted predecessor. If a question gives you 110 and 995, the 995 is the encrypted one and the 110 is the legacy.

SSH — one port, three jobs

SSH (port 22) is the exception that proves the rule. It came along later, was designed for security from day one, and it absorbed two earlier roles at once: secure remote shell (replacing Telnet on 23) and secure file transfer (SFTP and SCP all run over the same SSH connection). One port, three jobs.

If you've never SSH'd into a remote machine before, the way to think about it is: it's the secure version of "logging into a computer that isn't sitting in front of you," and on your home network it's how you'd reach your gaming PC from your Mac on the same LAN in the next room. The fact that all three SSH-based protocols share port 22 also means a question asking "what port does SCP use" has a less-obvious answer (22, not some new number).

DNS, DHCP, NTP, SNMP

The infrastructure protocols sit in their own cluster. is 53, and the gotcha the exam loves is that uses both UDP (for normal queries) and TCP (for zone transfers and any response too big for a UDP packet). is 67 and 68, with 67 the server side and 68 the client. If you've watched a laptop join a network and silently get an IP address, that's a conversation on those two ports. NTP is 123, and the reason it gets its own port even though "what time is it" sounds trivial is that without synchronized clocks you can't correlate logs across machines, which means you can't reconstruct an attack timeline. SNMP is 161 for queries and 162 for traps (where the device pushes an alert outward), and the security wrinkle is that v1 and v2 send community strings in cleartext, so v3 is the version you'd actually use on your home network.

LDAP and SMB

Then the directory and file-sharing services. LDAP is 389 in cleartext and 636 when wrapped in TLS — same -arrives-on-a-new-port pattern as the mail protocols. SMB is 445, the Windows file-sharing protocol, and the reason it shows up in security training is that EternalBlue and WannaCry both exploited it. If you've ever used a Windows shared folder over a network, that traffic was on 445.

Database ports (keep off the internet)

Database ports are worth knowing as a group rather than individually. MSSQL is 1433, Oracle is 1521, MySQL is 3306, Postgres is 5432. The thing the exam is testing isn't really "which database is on which port" but "do you know that none of these should be reachable from the internet." A question that describes a DB port open to 0.0.0.0 has a clear right answer: that's a misconfiguration regardless of which database is running.

RDP exposed = bad day

Finally, RDP is 3389. Picture the Windows remote-desktop session where someone's mouse is moving on a screen they're not physically near. It's been the entry point for an enormous number of incidents, partly because organizations exposed it directly, and partly because brute-forcing weak admin passwords against an open RDP port is a profitable industry. Every "RDP exposed" question has the same right answer: put it behind a or zero-trust gateway.

The third is to design the system as if the perimeter has already been compromised, which forces every component to authenticate and authorize independently rather than assuming the firewall has done that work.

is a specific DoS pattern at L4. Recall that TCP connections start with SYN, then SYN/ACK, then ACK. A sends the SYN, ignores the SYN/ACK reply, and never sends the final ACK, so the server is left holding a half-open connection waiting on a handshake that's never coming. Repeat that a few thousand times a second and the server's TCP backlog fills up, and real connections start getting refused. The defense — SYN cookies — is clever: instead of allocating a connection slot when the SYN arrives, the server encodes everything it needs to know into the SYN/ACK and only allocates state if the final ACK ever shows up. No half-open state, no flood.

They can read it, change it, or both. The classic version is a coffee-shop wifi attack: the attacker stands up a network with the same SSID as the cafe, your laptop joins it without asking you, and now everything you do flows through their machine. is the answer the exam wants because it makes the data unreadable to the attacker even when they're sitting in the middle, and HSTS plus pinning make it harder for them to convince your browser to accept their fake certificate.

is the L2 cousin of . ARP is how a machine on a LAN figures out "what MAC address is at this IP address." Picture a sticky-note board on the network where every machine writes "192.168.1.10 is at aa:bb:cc..." When an attacker on the same network keeps shouting "no actually, that IP is at MY ," the other machines update their notes and start sending traffic to the attacker. The defense — Dynamic ARP Inspection — is the network equivalent of "the switch refuses sticky notes that don't match the lease records." A managed switch on a segmented LAN can do this; a cheap unmanaged switch cannot, and that's why segmenting onto a separate VLAN matters: it limits the blast radius of L2 attacks to just that segment.

attacks the lookup itself. When you type bank.com into your browser, your computer asks a resolver to translate that to an IP address. If an attacker can corrupt the resolver's cache so that bank.com points to *their* server, you get a perfect-looking page on the right URL. DNSSEC adds cryptographic signatures to records so the resolver can verify the answer is authentic, and DoH (DNS over HTTPS) and DoT (DNS over ) prevent eavesdropping on the query itself.

Replay attacks capture an authentication exchange and re-send it later, hoping it still works. Imagine intercepting your boss's "approve this transaction" message and sending it again next week to approve a different transaction. The defense is putting freshness into the protocol: a nonce (a one-time random number) or a timestamp that the receiver checks, so that a replay of yesterday's message is recognizable as stale.

Session hijacking goes after the cookie or token that proves you're already logged in. If an attacker can steal your session token after you've authenticated, they don't need your password — they have the bracelet, which is what the doorman is checking. Defenses focus on protecting the token in flight ( so it can't be sniffed), in storage (HttpOnly cookies the JavaScript can't read), and on rotating it often enough that a stolen one expires before it's useful.

Side-channel attacks infer a secret from something the system unintentionally leaks: timing of operations, power consumption, electromagnetic emissions, cache state. The Spectre and Meltdown attacks were cache-based side-channels. Constant-time cryptographic implementations exist specifically because non-constant-time code can leak the bits of a private key through how long it takes to compute things.

Zero-day is shorthand for a for which no patch exists yet, often because the vendor doesn't know about it. The defense is structural rather than specific: defense in depth, segmentation, that flags behavioral anomalies rather than known signatures, and minimizing attack surface so that even an unknown bug has fewer paths to exploitation.

Mnemonic · chunking — APT vs smash-and-grab

Chunk 1: Persistent = months, low-and-slow, many footholds. Chunk 2: Resourced = budget for bribes, tools, lawyers — not a teenager in a hoodie trope. Chunk 3: Detect = long-window SIEM hunts, not one alert. Exam: APT → hunting + segmentation, not “patch Tuesday only.”

APT (Advanced Persistent ) is the term for a well-resourced, patient adversary, usually a nation-state or organized-crime crew, that gets in quietly and stays for months. The defining word is *persistent*: they're not smash-and-grab. They establish multiple footholds, learn your environment, and exfiltrate slowly. Detection requires looking at long-time-horizon patterns rather than individual alerts, which is why threat hunting and correlation across long windows matters in mature programs.

Insider is the one that breaks most outside-in defenses, because the person already has legitimate access. It splits into malicious (the disgruntled employee copying customer lists) and negligent (the well-meaning employee who emailed a spreadsheet of PII to the wrong address). Least privilege and separation of duties limit how much damage either type can do; UEBA tools (User and Entity Behavior Analytics) try to spot the malicious version by flagging "this user is suddenly accessing things they've never touched."

is the canonical example of using all three at once, and it's worth tracing through. When your browser connects to an HTTPS site, the part (RSA or ECDH) does the slow but useful job of agreeing on a session key without ever sending it across the wire in the clear. Once both sides have that session key, the rest of the conversation runs on symmetric (AES) because it's fast enough to handle bulk traffic. Three primitives, one protocol, each used for what it's good at.

Data lives in three states, and the controls that protect each one look different:

  • At rest, on disk or in a database or in a backup. The defense is symmetric , usually AES, applied via full-disk (LUKS, BitLocker), tablespace , or transparent database-level .
  • In transit, moving across a network. Defended by for application traffic, IPSec for site-to-site or remote , and SSH for management.
  • In use, sitting in memory during processing. This is the hardest case, and the defenses are still maturing. Trusted execution enclaves (Intel SGX, AMD SEV, AWS Nitro Enclaves) try to isolate sensitive work even from privileged code on the same host, and homomorphic lets you compute on encrypted data without decrypting, though it's mostly a research tool for now.

5.3 Data destruction, with the lab-attacker in the room

In context

Builds on 5.2: destruction tier matches classification and media.

On the exam: Clear vs purge vs destroy; crypto-erase when FDE was used.

Core idea

Clear overwrites; purge resists lab recovery; destroy the media; crypto-erase kills the key.

Analogy

Shred paper (clear), degauss drive (purge), smash platter (destroy), burn the only key to a safe (crypto-erase).

The reason there are three categories of destruction (clear, purge, destroy) is that they answer three different questions about who you're worried about getting your data afterward. The exam tests this distinction by giving you a scenario and asking which method is sufficient. The trick to answering reliably is to picture the model.

Clear means overwriting the data with zeros, ones, or random bytes. The drive is reusable, and a normal user (or a normal forensics tool from the consumer market) won't recover anything useful afterward. But "clear" only protects against software-level recovery. A lab with the right equipment can sometimes pull data off the drive's magnetic surface even after a single overwrite, especially on older HDDs. So clear is fine when you're handing the drive to another department in your own organization, where the model is "Bob in finance shouldn't accidentally see HR data." Clear is *not* fine when the drive is leaving your control.

Purge renders the data unrecoverable even by a well-equipped lab. There are three common methods. Degaussing uses a powerful magnetic field to scramble the magnetic domains on a hard drive, and it works on traditional spinning disks but not on SSDs (which don't store data magnetically). ATA Secure Erase is a command built into modern drives that triggers an internal wipe more thorough than a software overwrite. Crypto-erase is the elegant one: if the data was encrypted from the moment it was first written, you don't need to scrub the bits at all. You just destroy the key. The ciphertext that remains is mathematically indistinguishable from random data, so even if a lab recovers every bit perfectly, what they recover is noise. This is why full-disk is good practice even for drives that aren't being decommissioned: when you do decommission, the disposal step becomes "delete the key," and that takes seconds.

Destroy means physical destruction. Shredders that turn drives into confetti, incinerators that melt them, pulverizers that crush platters into powder. The drive isn't reusable, and the data isn't either. This is the option the exam wants when the data was classified Top Secret or its corporate equivalent, because anything less leaves the question "but what if?" unanswered. The Department of Defense for the most sensitive data calls for both crypto-erase *and* physical destruction; you don't get to pick.

Where the categories trip people up: questions sometimes describe a scenario like "your organization is selling decommissioned laptops to employees," and ask what level of sanitization is required. If those laptops contained Confidential data, clear isn't enough (the laptop is leaving the building, and the next owner's model includes them being mildly motivated and Google-savvy), but destroy is overkill (the drive should be reusable). Purge is the right answer. The exam likes this kind of "match the method to the data classification" pattern because it forces you to think about who you're protecting against, not just what the methods are.

5.6 Change management

In context

Builds on 5.5: changes are how misconfigurations enter production.

On the exam: Change management: authorize, test, back out, document.

Core idea

No cowboy production edits — CAB, maintenance window, rollback plan.

Analogy

  2. Review and approval. CAB or peer review, plus a classification.
  3. Test. Staging environment, regression coverage, a rollback drill.
  4. Implement. Within an agreed change window, on-call coverage in place.
  5. Document. What actually changed against what was requested, including the deltas.

5.9 Social engineering, told as the moments these attacks actually happen

In context

Builds on 5.8: social engineering bypasses technical controls via authorization paths.

On the exam: Social engineering: pretexting, phishing, vishing, tailgating — match tactic to story.

Core idea

Attack the human authorization path, not the firewall.

Analogy

Fake IT call asking for your MFA code — that's vishing plus urgency, not malware.

Social engineering questions on the exam are almost always written as a short story about a person who fell for something, and they ask you to name the technique. Memorizing terms in isolation doesn't help; recognizing the *shape* of each attack does. Here's each one as a small scene.

Pretexting is a phone call from someone who claims to be from IT, sounds confident, knows your name and your manager's name (because both are on LinkedIn), and asks you to verify your password "for a security check." The whole attack rests on a fabricated scenario that gives the request a plausible reason to exist. The defense isn't really technical — it's the that says IT will never ask for your password, plus a culture where pushing back on a confident-sounding stranger isn't rude.

Baiting is the USB drive labeled "Q4 Salaries" that someone left in the parking lot. Curiosity does the rest. Modern variants include tempting downloads ("you've won a gift card, click to claim") and infected QR codes stuck on top of legitimate ones. The defining feature is that the victim picks up the bait themselves — the attacker is just waiting.

Tailgating is when someone walks through a badge-controlled door right behind an authorized employee, without scanning their own badge. They don't ask, they just follow. Piggybacking is the same physical move, except the authorized employee *holds the door* for them, often because the follower is carrying coffee and a laptop bag and looks busy.

The exam treats these as different terms because the consent matters: tailgating is uninvited, piggybacking is inadvertently invited. Both are physical breaches, and both are why mantraps exist.

Quid pro quo is "I'll fix your computer problem if you let me into the system." The attacker offers a small service in exchange for access. A famous version: an attacker calls a string of internal extensions claiming to be from the help desk, eventually finding someone who actually does have a printer problem, and "helping" them — by walking them through steps that install remote-access software.

Dumpster diving is exactly what it sounds like. Organizations throw away surprising things: org charts, draft contracts, sticky notes with passwords, hard drives that weren't sanitized. An attacker doing reconnaissance on a target will sometimes find more from the dumpster than from the network. This is why shred-don't-toss policies are real policies.

Shoulder surfing is the person standing too close to you on the train who watches you type your phone PIN. Or the colleague who happens to walk past your desk while you're entering a password. Privacy screens on laptops exist for this reason. So does the convention of typing passwords with the screen turned slightly away.

Phishing, , and smishing are the same lure-by-message attack on three different channels: email, voice call, and SMS. has been having a renaissance because attackers now use deepfake audio to make a CEO's voice say "wire $200K to this vendor." exploits the fact that people trust SMS more than email, partly because they're used to receiving legitimate alerts there.

The lure is usually higher-stakes (a fake legal , a wire transfer request that appears to come from a CEO to a CFO), and the targeting is more careful. The reason it gets its own name is that the controls that protect normal employees often don't reach executives — they have admin assistants reading their email, exceptions to enforcement, and authority to override "in special cases."

Example: Palo Alto policy by application ID, not just port.

On the exam: Beyond port/protocol — application identity.

NIST

US National Institute of Standards and Technology — publishes frameworks and SPs (800-61 IR, 800-63B passwords, 800-207 zero trust).

Used for: Baseline controls, IR phases, and modern password guidance cited on the exam.

Prevalence: Very common on CC when stem says “best practice” or names NIST. inferred

Example: Six-phase IR lifecycle from SP 800-61.

On the exam: Prefer NIST over legacy “rotate every 90 days” when both appear.

Non-repudiation

Proves who did what so they cannot credibly deny it — signatures, audit trails.

Used for: Contracts, financial approvals, and forensic accountability.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Signed PDF with private key; correlated user ID in logs.

On the exam: Not the same as confidentiality.

OAuth 2.0

Authorization framework — delegated access without sharing the user’s password (identity often via OpenID Connect).

Used for: “Login with Google,” API scopes, and third-party app access to resources.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC; auth vs authz distinction is tested. inferred

Analogy: Valet key: starts car, cannot open trunk.

Example: Mobile game requests read-only access to your contacts — not your Google password.

On the exam: OAuth authorizes; OIDC authenticates.

PCI DSS

Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard — protect cardholder data environments.

Used for: Retail/e-commerce segmentation, logging, and crypto for payment data.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC when payments appear. inferred

Example: Isolate CDE; never store full track data.

On the exam: Scope reduction is a recurring theme.

Phishing

Social engineering via deceptive messages to steal credentials or deliver malware.

Used for: Initial access in many breaches; pairs with email controls and MFA.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: Fake invoice link to a credential harvester.

On the exam: Human factor — technical control is filter + MFA + training.

PKI / CA

Public Key Infrastructure — CAs bind identities to public keys; CRL/OCSP handle revocation.

Used for: TLS trust chains, code signing, and smart-card login.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Analogy: Passport office for digital certificates.

Example: Browser validates HTTPS cert chain to a trusted root.

On the exam: Chain-of-trust and revocation questions.

Policy

High-level management mandate — what the organization must achieve.

Used for: Sets direction for standards, procedures, and audits.

Prevalence: Very common on CC document hierarchy. inferred

Example: “All laptops must use full-disk encryption.”

On the exam: Broadest document; not step-by-step.

Procedure

Step-by-step instructions operators follow to meet a standard.

Used for: Runbooks, provisioning workflows, and IR checklists.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: Ticket steps to grant VPN access after manager approval.

On the exam: Operational “how”; not the same as regulation.

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts or exfiltrates data to extort payment — hits availability and often confidentiality.

Used for: Backup testing, IR playbooks, and segmentation.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: Encrypted file shares plus leak threat if unpaid.

On the exam: Often maps to A and sometimes C on CIA questions.

RBAC

Role-Based Access Control — permissions attach to job roles; users inherit via role membership.

Used for: Enterprise apps, cloud IAM, and least-privilege by job function.

Prevalence: Very common on CC access-control questions. inferred

Analogy: Hospital badges: nurse vs doctor, not per-person spreadsheets.

Example: “Analyst” role can read reports; “Admin” can change settings.

On the exam: Contrast with ABAC when attributes (time, device) matter.

Regulation

Legally binding rules from government or industry bodies.

Used for: Compliance programs, fines, and contractual obligations.

Prevalence: Common on CC privacy/compliance. inferred

Example: GDPR fines; HIPAA enforcement for PHI mishandling.

On the exam: Match regulatory driver in stem to control domain.

Reverse proxy

Server-facing proxy that accepts internet connections and forwards to internal apps (nginx, Cloudflare origin).

Used for: TLS termination, load balancing, WAF, and hiding internal topology.

Prevalence: Common on CC network/application stems. inferred

Analogy: Receptionist who takes all visitor calls then rings the right office.

Example: Public nginx on DMZ VLAN forwards only to app tier on port 8443.

On the exam: Contrast with forward proxy (user outbound).

Risk

Potential for loss when a threat exploits a vulnerability affecting an asset — managed through identify, assess, treat.

Used for: Risk registers, treatment decisions, and quantitative models (SLE/ALE).

Prevalence: Very common on CC domain 1. inferred

Example: SLE = AV × EF; ALE = SLE × ARO; treat via avoid/transfer/mitigate/accept.

On the exam: Keep threat vs vulnerability vs risk straight.

Rootkit

Malware that hides its presence by hooking OS/kernel or firmware.

Used for: Persistent stealth and difficult eradication.

Prevalence: Light–moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Compromised driver hides files from Task Manager.

On the exam: Eradication may require rebuild, not just delete file.

RSA

Rivest–Shamir–Adleman — widely used asymmetric algorithm for key exchange and digital signatures.

Used for: TLS handshakes, cert keys, and legacy VPN.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Encrypt small AES session key with server RSA public key.

On the exam: ECC often preferred for new deployments (size/performance).

RTO / RPO

RTO = maximum acceptable downtime. RPO = maximum acceptable data loss (time since last good copy).

Used for: Backup frequency, replication, and DR tier design.

Prevalence: Very common on CC BC/DR. inferred

Example: RTO 4h: service back by 4pm; RPO 1h: lose at most one hour of transactions.

On the exam: Do not swap RTO and RPO on timed questions.

SAML

Security Assertion Markup Language — XML federation where an Identity Provider asserts identity to a Service Provider.

Used for: Browser-based enterprise SSO to cloud apps.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC; often contrasted with OAuth/OIDC. inferred

Example: Work Azure AD → Salesforce login without a second password.

On the exam: Look for “federation” and “assertion” wording.

SIEM

Security Information and Event Management — centralize logs, correlate events, alert analysts.

Used for: SOC triage, compliance reporting, and incident detection.

Prevalence: Very common on CC IR and operations stems. inferred

Analogy: Air-traffic control for security events.

Example: Correlate failed logins across VPN, AD, and firewall in one console.

On the exam: Pair with SOAR for automated playbooks.

Smishing

SMS phishing — lure links or replies via text.

Used for: MFA prompts, parcel scams, and mobile credential harvest.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Text says “package held” with a shortened URL.

On the exam: People often trust SMS more than email.

SOAR

Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response — playbooks that act on SIEM/ticket findings.

Used for: Faster containment and consistent IR steps.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Auto-quarantine host after high-confidence malware alert.

On the exam: Automation vs manual runbook when stem says “orchestrate.”

SOC

Security Operations Center — people and process monitoring, triaging, and escalating security events.

Used for: 24/7 watch, incident coordination, and metric reporting.

Prevalence: Common on CC (people/process, not a SKU). inferred

Example: Analyst investigates SIEM alert, opens ticket, calls IR lead.

On the exam: Distinguish SOC (team) from SIEM (product).

SOC 2

Service Organization Control 2 — AICPA trust criteria report (security, availability, confidentiality, etc.) for service providers.

Used for: Vendor due diligence for SaaS.

Prevalence: Light on CC but appears in cloud/vendor context. inferred

Example: Customer asks startup for SOC 2 Type II before storing data.

On the exam: Audit attestation, not a law like GDPR.

Social engineering

Manipulating people into breaking security — phishing, pretexting, baiting, tailgating.

Used for: Bypassing strong technology when humans comply.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: “IT support” call asking for MFA codes.

On the exam: Root cause when technology was fine.

Spear phishing

Targeted phishing aimed at specific people or roles using personal context.

Used for: Credential theft and malware delivery against high-value users.

Prevalence: Common on CC social-engineering stems. inferred

Example: Email references your real project name and manager.

On the exam: More targeted than bulk phishing; less broad than whaling alone.

Spyware

Covertly monitors activity — keystrokes, screens, exfiltration.

Used for: Privacy violation and credential theft.

Prevalence: Light–moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Keylogger sends typed passwords to attacker.

On the exam: Often confidentiality impact.

SSL

Secure Sockets Layer — legacy predecessor to TLS; colloquially “SSL” still means TLS in practice.

Used for: Historical term in VPN/product names (SSL VPN).

Prevalence: Moderate on CC — know TLS replaced SSL. inferred

Example: “SSL VPN” today usually runs TLS underneath.

On the exam: Modern best practice always says TLS, not SSLv3.

SSO

Single Sign-On — one authentication event unlocks multiple applications.

Used for: Cutting password fatigue and centralizing identity for SaaS and internal apps.

Prevalence: Common on CC with SAML/OAuth stems. inferred

Analogy: One hotel key for room, gym, and parking.

Example: Corporate IdP logs you into Slack and Jira without retyping passwords.

On the exam: Distinguish authentication (SSO) from authorization (what you can do inside each app).

Standard

Mandatory, specific requirements that implement policy.

Used for: Technology choices, crypto suites, and mandatory configurations.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: “TLS 1.2+ with AES-256-GCM only.”

On the exam: More specific than policy; less procedural than a procedure.

Symmetric encryption

Same key encrypts and decrypts — fast for bulk data; key distribution is the hard part.

Used for: Disk encryption, VPN bulk traffic, TLS session data after handshake.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: AES-GCM for TLS payload after keys are agreed.

On the exam: Contrast with asymmetric for key exchange.

SYN flood

DoS that fills TCP backlog with half-open connections by sending SYNs without completing handshake.

Used for: Availability attack on TCP services.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Thousands of SYNs/sec exhaust web server connection table.

On the exam: SYN cookies mitigate without holding full state.

Threat

Actor or event that could cause harm — attacker, insider, malware, disaster.

Used for: Threat modeling and control selection.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: Phishing campaign targeting finance approvers.

On the exam: Threat is who/what; vulnerability is the weakness they use.

TLS

Transport Layer Security — encrypts and authenticates traffic in transit (successor to SSL).

Used for: HTTPS, secure email hops, and API protection.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: Browser padlock; cert validates server identity.

On the exam: Confidentiality + integrity on the wire; cert errors matter.

Trojan

Malware disguised as legitimate software — does not self-replicate like a worm.

Used for: Initial access via “helpful” downloads.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Analogy: Trojan horse gift that hides soldiers inside.

Example: Fake PDF viewer installs backdoor.

On the exam: User installed it thinking it was safe.

Virus

Malware that attaches to files/programs and spreads when the host runs or shares infected files.

Used for: User-action propagation via attachments.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC malware taxonomy. inferred

Example: Macro-enabled document infects spreadsheets on open.

On the exam: Needs host file — contrast worm.

Vishing

Voice phishing — social engineering by phone or voicemail.

Used for: Credential theft, MFA fatigue, and deepfake voice scams.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Caller claims to be IT and asks for MFA codes.

On the exam: Same family as phishing; channel is voice.

VLAN

Virtual LAN — logical L2 broadcast domain on a switch, isolating traffic without new cables.

Used for: Guest Wi-Fi, IoT, and server segmentation.

Prevalence: Very common on CC network domain. inferred

Example: Guest VLAN cannot reach server VLAN without routed ACLs.

On the exam: Segmentation, not encryption by itself.

VPN

Virtual Private Network — encrypted tunnel over untrusted networks for remote or site-to-site access.

Used for: Remote work, vendor access, and site interconnect.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: IPsec or SSL VPN to corporate LAN from home.

On the exam: Protects wire confidentiality; still needs MFA and authorization.

Vulnerability

Weakness a threat can exploit — unpatched CVE, misconfiguration, weak policy.

Used for: Patching, hardening, and vulnerability management.

Prevalence: Very common on CC. inferred

Example: RDP exposed to internet without MFA.

On the exam: Controls reduce likelihood or impact, not the threat actor alone.

WAF

Web Application Firewall — HTTP-aware rules for OWASP-class attacks.

Used for: Protecting public web apps from SQLi, XSS, and bots.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: ModSecurity in front of nginx blocking SQLi patterns.

On the exam: Application layer — not a substitute for network ACLs alone.

Whaling

Spear phishing aimed at executives — wire fraud, fake approvals, high impact.

Used for: BEC and fraud losses.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: CEO impersonation email authorizing an urgent wire transfer.

On the exam: Executive + financial urgency → whaling.

Worm

Self-propagating malware across networks without user opening each copy.

Used for: Fast lateral movement and internet-scale outbreaks.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC. inferred

Example: Worm scans SMB and spreads automatically.

On the exam: Self-spreads — contrast trojan/virus.

WPA / WPA2 / WPA3

Wi-Fi Protected Access — wireless link encryption/authentication (WPA2-AES-CCMP common; WPA3 improves handshakes).

Used for: Securing wireless LANs against eavesdropping and rogue APs.

Prevalence: Moderate on CC wireless stems. inferred

Example: Corporate WPA2-Enterprise with 802.1X to RADIUS.

On the exam: WEP is obsolete; WPA2 CCMP is the exam-friendly cipher answer.

Zero Trust

Never trust by network location alone; verify every request; assume breach.

Used for: Micro-segmentation, strong identity, continuous validation.

Prevalence: Growing on CC (NIST SP 800-207 framing). inferred

Analogy: Check ID at every door, not just the lobby.

Example: MFA + device posture before admin API access, even “inside” the office.

On the exam: Identity-centric, not perimeter-only.
